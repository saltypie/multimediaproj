Okinawan History

Prehistoric Gusuku Ryukyu Modern

Prehistoric Times (<1050 AD)

The pregistoric era of Okinawa was characterised by the presence of hunter-gatherer societies such as the Minatogawa people and the Shellmiden cultures.


During this time proto Japonic happened to split into Proto-Japanese and Proto-Ryukyuan propagating the difference in language today.


During the Shellmiden settlements were mostly coastal and society depended on fishing, boar hunting, pottery and shell-fish


The late shell-midden saw a shift from pit dwellings to pillared elevated wood-structures


Religious or symbolic traditions likely began during this era but aren't yet archaeologically evidenced.

Hajichi Tattoos

Hajichi, or traditional hand tattoos, were a common form of body art among Okinawan women as a rite of passage and protection.

The Gusuku Period

The Gusuku Period saw the rise of local chieftains(Aji) and the construction of fortified stone castles.

This era is also associated withe the introduction of agriculture to the Ryukyuan way of life. Crops included rice, barley, and millet

Trade blossomed with China and Japan with the import of ceramics and export of mother-of-pearl

Proto Ryukyuan solidified during this era

Small scale wars were waged by Aji eventually culminating in the sanzan period towards the end of the era

Many surviving records of this era were in Chinese not Ryukyuan

In 1260-1299 a King known as Eiso introduced a formal tax system on grains, cloth and weapons.

Religion

Budhism is likely to have arrived in this era finding itself alongside existing nature worship and ancestor worship

Origins of Shisa

Shisa lion-dogs were introduced from China and became symbolic protectors of Okinawan households.

The Ryukyu Kingdom Era

This era marked a unified kingdom that prospered through maritime trade with neighboring countries.

A summary of the Ryukyu Kingdom Era

The first dynasty to unify the kingdom was the Sho dynasty initially under King Sho Hashi




From 1372 Ryukyu was a tributary state of China, Later invasions from Japan would make it a vassal of the Satsuma domain

Trade with regions such as Indonesia influenced the creation of Bingata textiles with bright colourful patterns

Musical arts and instruments such as the Sanshin developed with influence from the Chinese Sanxian.


The popular martial art of Karate formed as a result of local martial arts and Chinese martial arts influences.


Shisa were introduced at this time period likely from the Chinese shishi.

Hajichi Tattoos

Hajichi, or traditional hand tattoos, were a common form of body art among Okinawan women as a rite of passage and protection. They'd eventually be banned during Japanese occupation

Shuri Castle

Shuri Castle was the royal palace of the Ryukyu Kingdom and a center of government, culture, and diplomacy.

Modern Period

The rise of imperial Japan during the Meiji revolution saw the abolishment of the Ryukyu kingdom and annexation followed in 1879.

Assimilation, Annexation and Suppression

Following annexation schools enforced the Japanese language and forbade Ryukyuan languages. Local customs such as hajichi tattoos were deemed primitive and were stigmatised. Priestesses were suppressed and Japanese officials were planted in the administration

World War 2

The USA's operation iceberg planned to capture Okinawa as a stage for mainland invasion. On April 1945 forces began landing, fighting lasted 82 days with allied losses of ~12,000 and civilian losses of ~100,000(half the island's population). The Japanese military forced and coerced locals, children and elderly alike to commit suicide attacks against American forces. Anecdotes from Okinawan historians also detail atrocities such as rape by the American forces.


Post-war and Occupation Era

The US occupation era saw a potential return to Ryukuan languages however, educators and pro-Japanese Okinawans encourage the use of standard Japanese

In 1972 Okinawa was officially returned to Japan. Ryukyuan languages were nearly extinct with many either speaking Japanese or a mix of the two.

Revival

In 2000 the society for language revitalisation was launched and Ryukyuan languages were considered endangered by UNESCO in 2009. Shimakutuba day was launched in 2006 to promote Ryukyuan languages.

Okinawa Prefecture Flag

The Okinawa Prefecture flag symbolizes unity, progress and the Ocean.
